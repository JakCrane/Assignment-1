What is an orbit?

Orbits are paths, usually circular, that smaller space bodies follow around much larger bodies. It is what the moon does around earth and what the earth does around the sun. So how is it that satellites stay in orbit and dont just fall out of the sky?

Gravity is one of the 4 fundamental forces of the universe. It is a force that attracts two objects towards each other. The strength of gravity depends on the mass of the object (how heavy they are) and the distance between them. The more massive an object is, the stronger its gravity is. The farther apart two objects are, the weaker the gravity between them is. The most familiar example of gravity is the force that pulls objects towards the center of the Earth, which is what keeps us on the ground and what makes things fall.

Circular motion refers to the movement of an object in a circular path around a fixed point, known as the center of rotation. The object is constantly changing its direction as it moves along the circular path.

An important concept in circular motion is centripetal force, which is the force that acts towards the center of the circle and is responsible for keeping an object moving in a circle. In the case of an object on a string, the tension in the string will stop the object flying off. Counterintuitively satellites moving in a circle around the Earth recieve their centripetal force from gravity.

Circular motion is a common motion found in many real-world situations such as car wheels, roller coasters, and spinning amusement park rides.

Kepler was a German astronomer in the early 17th century. He used data collected by his mentor Tycho Brahe to put together 3 laws of planetary motion. Tycho Brache was widely considered to have recorded the most accurate astronomical data at the time giving keplar a massive advantage over other scientists of the era. This advantage keplar leveraged to disprove the geocentric model of the planets and really shake up the scientific community. The geocentric model can be seen on the right and while it looks like nonsense to us today it was accepted as truth until the 17th century.

The first law, (the law of orbits) states that shape of an orbit is an elipse with the sun at one of the two foci. The second law, (law of areas) is shown on the right and states that a line connecting a planet to the sun sweeps out equal areas in equal times. This means that a planet moves faster when it is closer to the sun and slower when it is farther away. The third law, (harmonic law) states that the further from a body you orbit the slower your speed must be.
