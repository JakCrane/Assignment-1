Design & Prototype

Preliminary design phase

The PDP is focused entirely around a problem to fix or a data set to collect. Lots of research and investigation occurs in this phase as they have alot of questions to answer if they want to pitch the idea successfully to recieve the funding. The questions they seek to answer include: if the goal of the project is feasible, what constraints exist for the project, if this is the best solution to acheive the goal and what are the associated risks or uncertainties with the project. At the end of the first phase there should be a rough idea of how much it will cost, how long it will take, how effective it is and what size or shape the satellite will be. Once all these questions have been answered they will be pitched to funding organisations and if successful the project will move onto the critical design phase. It is common for the PDP to be itterative as noone starts with a perfect idea first try.

Critical design phase

With relative confidence the chosen solution is ideal the focus turns to making it work in practice. The CDP is where exact plans and designs are drafted up. Everything is specified exactly how it will work and often prototypes are produced to test the critical parts of the design for flaws. The exact mass calculations, power consumption and interfacing between subsystems is also clarified. From the critical design, two seperate people building the designs should produce identical end products. often a prototype will be tested in simulated space environments.

Build & Test

Assembly and Integration

Bespoke parts of the project are often ordered from external companies as making them in house is usually more effort than it is worth. Once everything is collected in one room the assembly stage begins. This is done in a clean room to prevent contamination like dirt/dust from ruining the design. Then various components and subsystems of the satellite are put together. This includes assembling the structure of the satellite, such as the frame or platform that holds all the subsystems in place, as well as attaching the power, propulsion, communications, and scientific instruments. This stage also includes installing and testing the software and electronic systems that control the satellite's functions.

Launch & Operations

Launch

A satellite is launched into space on board a launch vehicle (lv), usually developed by an outside company. These launch vehicles are often rockets taking up many different payloads and involve multiple stages, each with their own engines and fuel tanks. These vehicles progressively increase the satellites altitude and vertical velocity until they are ready to release the satellites. The satellite is typically attached to the top of the launch vehicle, and protected by a fairing during the early stages of ascent to protect it from destructive G forces. The rocket is typically launched from a launch pad, and the trajectory is carefully controlled to ensure that the satellite is inserted into the desired orbit. The launch is controlled from a mission control center, where a team of engineers and technicians monitor the progress of the launch and make adjustments as needed.

Operations

This is the big moment. Where all the years of hard work come together in achieving the objective set out in the mission's design phase. The satellite control center usually sends commands to the satellite via radio waves and tells the payload section of the satellite to begin. Then the images or information collected is beamed back down to earth often along with system monitoring information to check the health of the satellite.
